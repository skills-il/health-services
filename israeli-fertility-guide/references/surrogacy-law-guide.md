# Israeli Surrogacy Law: Comprehensive Guide

## Overview

Israel was one of the first countries in the world to legalize and regulate surrogacy, enacting the Surrogacy Agreements Law (chok hapundakaut, חוק הפונדקאות) in 1996. The law has been amended several times, most recently to expand eligibility to include single individuals and same-sex couples. This guide covers the legal framework, process, and practical details.

## Legal Framework

### The Surrogacy Agreements Law (Chok HaPundakaut)

| Aspect | Details |
|--------|---------|
| Original law | Enacted 1996 |
| Key amendments | 2014 (expanded to single women), 2022 (expanded to single men and same-sex male couples) |
| Governing body | Ministry of Health, through the Approval Committee for Surrogacy Agreements |
| Type of surrogacy | Gestational surrogacy only (the surrogate has no genetic connection to the child) |
| Traditional surrogacy | Not permitted under Israeli law |

### Who Can Use Surrogacy in Israel

| Eligible Parties | Details |
|-----------------|---------|
| Married couples (man and woman) | Original eligibility group |
| Cohabiting couples (man and woman) | Added in amendments |
| Single women | Expanded eligibility |
| Single men | Expanded in 2022 amendment |
| Same-sex female couples | Expanded eligibility |
| Same-sex male couples | Expanded in 2022 amendment |

**Medical requirement:** The intended parent(s) must demonstrate a medical need for surrogacy. Common qualifying reasons include:
- Absence of a uterus (congenital or due to surgery)
- Medical condition making pregnancy dangerous
- Repeated IVF failure
- Recurrent miscarriages
- Single man or male couple (inherent biological need)

## The Approval Committee (Vaada Le'Ishur Heskemei Pundakaut)

All surrogacy agreements in Israel must be approved by a special committee before treatment begins.

### Committee Composition

| Member | Role |
|--------|------|
| Retired judge or equivalent (chair) | Legal oversight |
| Physician specializing in OB/GYN | Medical assessment |
| Clinical psychologist or social worker | Psychological assessment |
| Religious representative | Reviews religious compatibility |
| Public representative | Public interest |

### What the Committee Evaluates

| Criterion | What They Review |
|----------|-----------------|
| Medical necessity | Is surrogacy medically justified for the intended parents? |
| Surrogate suitability | Does the surrogate meet all legal requirements? |
| Agreement terms | Is the financial compensation fair and legal? |
| Psychological readiness | Have all parties undergone psychological evaluation? |
| Religious compatibility | Is there religious compatibility between surrogate and intended parents? (This requirement has been relaxed in recent amendments) |
| Legal representation | Do all parties have independent legal counsel? |

## Surrogate Requirements

The law sets strict criteria for who can serve as a surrogate (pundakait, פונדקאית).

| Requirement | Details |
|-------------|---------|
| Residency | Must be an Israeli resident |
| Age | 22-39 years old |
| Previous births | Must have given birth to at least one child |
| Marital status | Originally required to be single/divorced/widowed; married women may now qualify in certain circumstances |
| Genetic connection | Cannot be genetically related to the intended parents |
| Health | Must pass comprehensive medical and psychological evaluation |
| Maximum surrogacies | Generally limited to prevent excessive surrogacies (committee discretion) |
| Consent | Must provide fully informed, voluntary consent |

## The Surrogacy Process: Step by Step

### Phase 1: Preparation (2-6 months)

| Step | Action | Timeline |
|------|--------|----------|
| 1 | Consult with a surrogacy lawyer | Week 1-2 |
| 2 | Find a surrogate (independently or through an agency) | 1-6 months |
| 3 | Both parties undergo psychological evaluation | 2-4 weeks |
| 4 | Medical evaluation of the surrogate | 2-4 weeks |
| 5 | Draft the surrogacy agreement with independent lawyers | 2-4 weeks |

### Phase 2: Committee Approval (2-6 months)

| Step | Action | Timeline |
|------|--------|----------|
| 6 | Submit application to the Approval Committee | After all evaluations complete |
| 7 | Committee review of documentation | 4-12 weeks |
| 8 | Committee hearing (all parties may be called) | Scheduled by committee |
| 9 | Committee decision | Usually within 2-4 weeks of hearing |

### Phase 3: Medical Treatment (2-4 months)

| Step | Action | Timeline |
|------|--------|----------|
| 10 | Begin IVF process for the intended mother (or egg donor) | Immediately after approval |
| 11 | Egg retrieval and embryo creation | 2-4 weeks |
| 12 | Prepare surrogate's uterus with hormones | 2-3 weeks |
| 13 | Embryo transfer to surrogate | Day 3 or 5 after fertilization |
| 14 | Pregnancy test | 14 days after transfer |

### Phase 4: Pregnancy (9 months)

| Step | Action | Timeline |
|------|--------|----------|
| 15 | Ongoing prenatal care for the surrogate | Throughout pregnancy |
| 16 | Intended parents may attend appointments (by agreement) | Throughout pregnancy |
| 17 | Prepare for birth and legal procedures | Final trimester |

### Phase 5: Birth and Legal Transfer (1-2 months)

| Step | Action | Timeline |
|------|--------|----------|
| 18 | Birth | As scheduled or natural |
| 19 | Parentage order (tzav horut, צו הורות) issued by family court | Within days of birth |
| 20 | Birth certificate issued in intended parents' names | After court order |
| 21 | Post-birth follow-up for surrogate | As needed |

## Rights of All Parties

### Intended Parents' Rights

| Right | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Parentage | Legal parents from the moment of the court order |
| Birth certificate | Child registered to the intended parents |
| Decision-making | Medical decisions during pregnancy (within reason and agreement terms) |
| Attendance at birth | Usually included in the agreement |

### Surrogate's Rights

| Right | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Compensation | Fair financial compensation as defined in the agreement |
| Medical care | All medical expenses covered by intended parents |
| Independent legal counsel | Must have her own lawyer, separate from the intended parents |
| Withdrawal | Can withdraw consent before embryo transfer (not after) |
| Post-birth support | Psychological support and medical follow-up covered |
| Employment protection | Cannot be fired from her job due to serving as a surrogate |
| Health insurance | Full coverage during and after pregnancy |

### Child's Rights

| Right | Details |
|-------|---------|
| Legal parentage | Established through court order, not through the surrogate |
| Birth certificate | Issued to intended parents |
| Citizenship | Israeli citizenship if intended parents are Israeli |
| Knowledge of origins | The law does not currently require disclosure, but records are maintained |

## Cost Breakdown

Surrogacy in Israel involves significant out-of-pocket costs, though some medical expenses may be partially covered by the health basket.

| Expense Category | Approximate Cost (NIS) | Notes |
|-----------------|----------------------|-------|
| Legal fees (intended parents' lawyer) | 30,000-50,000 | Drafting agreement, committee application, court proceedings |
| Legal fees (surrogate's lawyer) | 10,000-20,000 | Paid by intended parents |
| Surrogate compensation | 80,000-120,000 | Monthly payments during pregnancy + lump sum at birth |
| Surrogate medical expenses | 10,000-30,000 | Beyond what health basket covers |
| Agency fee (if used) | 20,000-40,000 | Optional; matching and coordination services |
| Psychological evaluations | 5,000-10,000 | Both parties |
| IVF costs | Covered by health basket | If within 2-live-birth entitlement |
| Court fees | 2,000-5,000 | Parentage order |
| Insurance/contingency | 10,000-20,000 | Complications, additional transfers |
| **Total estimated range** | **150,000-250,000** | **Varies significantly by circumstances** |

## Religious Considerations

The original law included significant religious oversight. While this has been relaxed in recent amendments, some considerations remain.

| Aspect | Current Status |
|--------|---------------|
| Religious compatibility requirement | Reduced but not eliminated; committee considers this |
| Jewish law (Halacha) implications | A child born to a Jewish surrogate is considered Jewish by Orthodox authorities; if the surrogate is not Jewish, the child's status may be questioned |
| Rabbinate marriage registry | May require additional documentation for children born through surrogacy |
| Practical impact | Most surrogacy agreements still match religious backgrounds to avoid future complications |

## Common Challenges and Solutions

| Challenge | Solution |
|-----------|---------|
| Long wait for surrogate match | Consider working with an agency; expand geographic search |
| Committee rejection | Address specific concerns raised; reapply with additional documentation |
| Surrogate pregnancy complications | Agreement should include clear terms for medical decisions and additional compensation |
| Multiple transfer failures | Committee approval allows multiple attempts; discuss protocol changes with fertility specialist |
| Cost burden | Some non-profit organizations offer financial assistance; payment plans may be negotiated |

## Important Notes

1. **International surrogacy:** Israeli citizens who pursue surrogacy abroad face a different legal process for bringing the child to Israel and establishing parentage. This requires a separate court procedure.

2. **Egg donation in surrogacy:** If the intended mother cannot provide eggs, an egg donor may be used. The surrogate cannot be the egg donor (this is a legal requirement to prevent traditional surrogacy).

3. **Sperm donation in surrogacy:** If needed (e.g., for a single woman), sperm donation can be combined with surrogacy, though this adds complexity to the committee application.

4. **Timeline expectation:** From the decision to pursue surrogacy to the birth of a child, the typical timeline is 1.5 to 3 years. Patience and thorough preparation are essential.
